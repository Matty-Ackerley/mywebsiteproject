1.1 - DESCRIBING THE PURPOSE OF "IT" IN MODERN BUSINESS:

Information Technology can serve many different purposes in modern business. To name a few of the most obvious to me would be Communication, Efficiency and the Systems that IT allows.

It provides an ever-growing range of methods for companies to communicate with employees and customers. These can include websites, e-mail and online video or audio calls (variety of programs). This is why most smart phones or computers you can buy on the market today come with a range of these products already installed and ready to use. Easier communication makes the completion of a task or an order/inquiry so much quicker and more efficient than if you were limited to, for example, a face to face meeting. The ability to check in constantly and receive an update of the progress ensures that the task is being completed correctly, on time and to the standards agreed.

The use of systems is wide-ranging and can be anything from stock control, data storage and recall (not limited to being in the office) and MIS (management information systems) that allow companies to monitor and display data from sales, costs and timescales. [1]

1.7 - DESCRIBE LEGAL GUIDELINES AND CONSTRAINTS THAT IMPACT DIGITAL PROJECTS – HOW DOES GDPR IMPACT DIGITAL PROJECTS?

The GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) is something that most people have heard of but may not completely understand what it is. It consists of the following rights: [9]

  • The right of access (the right to view all data a company has collected on you and how it has been used).
  • The right to be forgotten (this can mean either your data is not collected and stored to begin with or that you withdraw permission for a company to store and use your data).
  • The right of data portability (the right to transfer your data from one company to another using industry recognised standards).
  • The right to be informed (a company needs to inform you that your information will be gathered so that you can give explicit permission for this to be done as this cannot be assumed).
  • The right to have information corrected (this can be either the correction of records that may be wrong or incompleted/out of date).
  • The right to restrict processing (this would mean that a company cannot use your data for processing but may hold onto the record of it, so long as it is not used).
  • The right to object (a company must always let you know that you have the right to object at the start of any form of communication). This right also allows you to have a company stop gathering information on you and it must be honoured as soon as the request is made.
  • The right to be notified (in the event of a data breach such as a hack, the company must inform you within 72 hours of them becoming aware if your data may have been compromised).
Keeping in line with our theme of a website as an example of a digital project, a website would have to notify any customers if their information would be stored as a result. It would entail how the individual used the site and for how long.

1.5 - DESCRIBING COPYRIGHT AND OTHER CONSTRAINTS AFFECTING WEBSITES.

As far as copyright and websites are concerned you should always assume that anything you see on the internet is copyrighted unless you are told otherwise. Any content that is of an original nature (be it images, written works, audio etc) falls under the Copyright law. Many countries have slight differences (for example in the US logos on a website are protected, while in the UK you would need to register them as trademarks [10]). There are exceptions such as educational usage that would allow limited personal use.

Another common constraint on website design would be the end-audience and how they will view the website. This can be from the device they will use (PC, console, mobile) to the size of screen they will use or even the level of zoom they will apply to the website (some browsers scale the images with the text while some do not [11]). This all has to be considered in the planning stage of the website.

1.6 - DESCRIBING ACCESS ISSUES THAT NEED TAKING INTO ACCOUNT - DESCRIBE ISSUES THAT PEOPLE WITH DISABILITIES MAY FACE WHEN USING WEB PAGES AND HOW WE CAN OVERCOME THEM?

A website should always consider the audience that will use it and the necessity for any disabilities such as visual impairment. There are many methods to aid in this, such as an option to enlarge the text size or audio description/commentation. These do not need to be enabled as default but the option to use them should always be there and be clear on how to use it. Websites without this kind of aid may find that people with disabilities are unable to use certain parts of the site, or access key information.

1.7 - DESCRIBE WHICH ARE THE APPROPRIATE FILE TYPES FOR WEBSITES.

Deciding upon the most appropriate file type for your website really boils down to what it is you are trying to display. A few examples are described below.

If you wanted to display a plain image, then a JPEG would be the most appropriate due to its small file sizes that will not slow down your website loading. If you wanted to include a picture with a transparent background then a PNG would be best as JPEGs do not allow this. One drawback with PNGs is that not all browsers support them and on those that do not the image would not load.

For small animated graphics or animated logos a GIF would be suitable, for longer video and the sort .MP4 would be an option though it would usually be advisable to upload the file to a website like youtube and embed it onto your website.

For audio files .MP3 is the most commonly used as this allows a small downloadable file size. [12]
